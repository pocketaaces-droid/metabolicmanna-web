The progression

What each of the seven days looks like.

Most beginner fasting plans dump you straight into a 16-hour fast on Day 1 and wonder why you quit by Day 3. This one ramps — one hour at a time — because the research says your body needs about a week to adapt.

Day Focus Window The action
Day 1 The overnight baseline 12:12 Finish dinner by 7pm, don't eat again until 7am. You've probably done this accidentally most nights of your life. Prove to yourself the rhythm is doable.
Day 2 Extend by two hours 14:10 Push the first meal from 7am to 9am. Drink water or black coffee until then. Notice the first hunger wave — it passes in about 15 minutes.
Day 3 Hydration + electrolytes 14:10 Same window. Add a pinch of salt to your morning water. Most of what beginners think is hunger is actually dehydration or low sodium.
Day 4 The adjustment day 14:10 Hold the window. Expect energy fluctuations — this is the glycogen-depletion phase where the body starts switching to stored fat for fuel. Completely normal.
Day 5 Push to 15 hours + food order 15:9 First meal at 10am. Focus on protein and fiber — and eat them before any carbs in the meal. Research shows this simple sequencing reduces blood sugar spikes by 29-54% with the same exact food.
Day 6 The target pattern 16:8 First meal at noon, last meal by 8pm. This is the most research-backed fasting window — from here, it's about maintenance, not extension.
Day 7 Reflection + sustain plan 16:8 Hold 16:8 and reflect on the week. Write down what worked, what didn't, and which version of the pattern you're keeping. The guide walks you through this step by step.

Quick answers

Before you sign up.

Short, direct answers to the four things people always ask first.

Is it really free?
Yes. Enter your email, get the PDF. No credit card, no upsell, no hidden tier. The guide is free because it's the top of our funnel — we build trust first, you decide later if you want anything else.
Is this safe for beginners?
The plan starts with a 12-hour overnight fast — something most people already do without thinking about it — and ramps up gradually to 16:8 over seven days. It is not safe if you are pregnant, have a history of eating disorders, type 1 diabetes, or take medications with food. Always consult your healthcare provider before starting any fasting protocol.
What's the science behind this plan?
The 7-day progression is based on research showing that the body needs roughly 7–14 days to adapt to longer fasting windows — a period called the metabolic switch. Each day in the plan is calibrated to match that adaptation curve, drawing on peer-reviewed work including de Cabo & Mattson (NEJM 2019), Sutton et al. (Cell Metab 2018), and the 2024 TIMET trial showing 10-hour TRE improves metabolic markers without significant lean muscle loss. We're honest about what's strong evidence (insulin sensitivity, blood pressure, metabolic flexibility) and what's still uncertain in humans (the popular "autophagy at 16 hours" claim).
Will fasting alone help me lose weight?
The honest answer: when calories are matched, intermittent fasting and continuous calorie restriction produce equivalent weight loss — not superior. The real reason to fast is metabolic: improved insulin sensitivity, blood pressure, and lipid markers that show up independent of the scale. If pure weight loss is your only goal, fasting won't outperform "just eating less." If you want to fix the underlying metabolism, fasting is one of the most-studied tools we have.
What if it doesn't work for me?
Then stop. The plan is a tool, not a test of character. If you feel dizzy, exhausted beyond normal, or just miserable after three days, break the fast and reassess. Not every protocol fits every body — and fasting is one of the ones that genuinely doesn't work for everyone. That's honest, not a bug.
