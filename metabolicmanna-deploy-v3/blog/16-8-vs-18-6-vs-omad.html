16:8 vs 18:6 vs OMAD vs 5:2 — which protocol fits you.

8 min read · Published April 14, 2026 · Fundamentals cluster
The short answer

The strongest evidence is behind 16:8 and 10-hour TRE — both are well-studied, sustainable, and metabolically effective. Tighter protocols like OMAD lack head-to-head evidence that they outperform 16:8, and the tradeoffs (muscle loss, difficulty hitting protein, adherence) get steeper the shorter the window. The best protocol is the one you'll actually keep.

"Intermittent fasting" is a catch-all term, not a single protocol. The four most-studied patterns have different demands, different outcomes, and very different learning curves. If you've been trying to figure out which one to start with, this is the honest research-backed comparison.

Two things to know up front: first, head-to-head clinical trials comparing specific fasting schedules against each other are rare — most of what you'll read online is opinion dressed as science. Second, the most replicated finding in the whole body of IF research is that adherence beats optimization. The protocol that works is the one you can stick with for months, not the one that sounds most hardcore on Reddit.

16:8 — the default, and the one with the best evidence.

16:8 means you fast for 16 hours and eat all your meals within an 8-hour window. It's the most-studied version of intermittent fasting in humans, and it's the one with the best safety profile, the best adherence data, and the best ratio of benefit to disruption.

The TIMET trial (Manoogian, Wilkinson et al., 2024, Annals of Internal Medicine) looked at a slightly gentler 10-hour version of this pattern in adults with metabolic syndrome. It produced reductions in body weight, BMI, and abdominal fat — without significant lean muscle mass loss. That "without significant muscle loss" is crucial: it's the line that tighter protocols struggle to hold.

And when Sutton et al., 2018 in Cell Metabolism ran a controlled-feeding trial of an even tighter 6-hour early-TRE window in prediabetic men — with calories matched so nobody lost weight — participants still improved insulin sensitivity, β-cell responsiveness, and blood pressure. Tighter windows can work, but 16:8 already captures most of the effect most of the time.

Best for: Beginners, anyone with a normal work schedule, anyone who wants to try IF without restructuring their whole life. If you're starting out, start here.

18:6 — moderate, if 16:8 feels too easy.

18:6 shrinks the eating window to 6 hours. The metabolic signal gets a little stronger, and it moves you closer to the tighter Sutton-style protocols — but the tradeoffs start appearing: harder to hit protein targets, more hunger management, more social scheduling friction.

There is no head-to-head human trial showing that 18:6 produces meaningfully better outcomes than 16:8 for metabolic markers. The case for pushing to 18:6 is largely about reaching a slightly deeper ketogenic state and (for some people) easier calorie control. It's a reasonable step up if 16:8 feels like your baseline. It is not a step up that most people need.

Best for: Anyone who has been doing 16:8 for a month or more and wants to push further. Not a starting point.

OMAD — one meal a day (23:1).

OMAD is a 23-hour fast with a single 1-hour eating window. It sounds efficient. It is not, in any clinical sense, superior to 16:8 for most outcomes — and the tradeoffs get steep.

  • Muscle preservation gets harder. One meal a day makes it incredibly difficult to hit adequate daily protein (roughly 0.7–1g per pound of lean body mass). The lean mass you lose when the eating window gets too tight is the lean mass that was helping your metabolic rate in the first place.
  • Side effects are more pronounced. Documented issues include extreme hunger, fatigue, irritability, nausea, difficulty concentrating, and cold extremities. These are not rare; they are common at this window length.
  • Adherence crashes. Clinical trials consistently show dropout rates are higher for more extreme protocols than for milder ones.
  • No head-to-head evidence of superiority. There is no RCT showing OMAD outperforms 16:8 on the outcomes that matter — insulin sensitivity, metabolic flexibility, fat mass, or sustained weight loss.

Best for: Honestly? Advanced practitioners, frequent travelers who need meal simplicity, or people doing it for specific religious or personal reasons with full knowledge of the tradeoffs. Not most people. Not beginners. And not anyone trying to build or preserve muscle.

Adherence beats optimization.

5:2 — five normal days, two restricted days.

5:2 is a different pattern entirely. You eat normally five days a week, and on two non-consecutive days you eat ~500–600 calories. It's easier to plan around social events (there are no daily eating windows) and research shows the outcomes for weight and metabolic markers are broadly similar to daily TRE.

The Trepanowski et al. (2017) trial in JAMA Internal Medicine compared alternate-day fasting against daily calorie restriction and found the weight loss was essentially equivalent. When calories are matched, IF is not uniquely superior — it's a different delivery mechanism for the same energy deficit.

One 2025 finding complicates the picture in an interesting way: a network meta-analysis of 56 RCTs in Current Nutrition Reports found that modified alternate-day fasting produced the biggest reductions in body weight (−5.18 kg) and waist circumference (−3.55 cm) across all IF modalities. The differences between protocols were modest, but if you're optimizing specifically for weight and waist, modified ADF has a slight edge in the latest data.

Best for: People who prefer flexibility over daily discipline, people whose schedules make daily eating windows hard, or people specifically targeting body composition changes. Requires more planning than 16:8.

Who should avoid each protocol entirely.

This is the section most "which protocol" content skips, and it's the one that matters most.

Do not start any fasting protocol — especially the more aggressive ones — without direct medical supervision if you have any of the following:

  • Pregnancy or active breastfeeding
  • Type 1 diabetes or insulin-dependent type 2 diabetes
  • A current or historical eating disorder of any kind
  • Blood pressure, blood sugar, or thyroid medications that must be taken with food
  • GLP-1 medications (semaglutide, tirzepatide) — these already reduce calorie intake 30–40%, so adding strict fasting creates severe risk of low energy availability. Stick to 12:12 at most.
  • Children and adolescents — growing bodies need consistent nutrition
  • Frail elderly adults or anyone underweight
  • Active recovery from illness, surgery, or injury

A note for women: The fasting research is dominated by male subjects, and women may be more sensitive to energy restriction than men. Aggressive protocols (OMAD, ADF, long fasts) can disrupt sex hormones and contribute to amenorrhea, sleep issues, and reduced bone density. Most women are better off with 16:8 or a 14:10. If you lose your period on a fasting protocol, stop immediately.

The protocol comparison table.

At a glance
16:8 — 16h fast / 8h eating. Strong human evidence (TIMET 2024, Sutton 2018). Best for beginners. Sustainable.
18:6 — 18h fast / 6h eating. Moderate evidence. Step up if 16:8 feels easy. No head-to-head superiority.
OMAD — 23h fast / 1h eating. Weakest sustainability evidence. Steep muscle/protein tradeoff. Not for beginners.
5:2 — 5 normal days, 2 restricted. Equivalent to daily TRE when calories matched. Needs more planning.
Modified ADF — The 2025 network meta-analysis winner for weight & waist, but by modest margins.

What's safe to believe vs. what to flag.

Safe to believe

  • 16:8 and 10-hour TRE have the strongest human evidence
  • Adherence matters more than window length
  • When calories are matched, IF ≈ regular calorie restriction for weight
  • Modified ADF has a slight edge for weight/waist in recent meta-analysis
  • OMAD's tradeoffs get steeper as the window shrinks

Flag as unproven

  • "OMAD is metabolically superior to 16:8" — no head-to-head evidence
  • "Tighter windows always produce more autophagy" — the human data doesn't support this
  • Any protocol claim made without noting the muscle/adherence tradeoffs
  • "IF is better than regular dieting" — only when calories differ
  • Aggressive protocols for women without acknowledging hormone risks

The honest bottom line.

If you take one thing from this post, take this: the best protocol is the one you actually stick with. Start with 16:8. Run it for a month. If it feels sustainable and you want to push further, step up to 18:6. Most people should stop there — the marginal gains from tighter windows are small and the tradeoffs (muscle, mood, adherence) are real.

Jumping straight to OMAD in week one is the most common way beginners fail at fasting. Don't do it. The research doesn't support it, the clinical literature doesn't support it, and your body won't thank you for it. Build the base, then build from the base.

If you're not sure how to start, read our companion post on how to ease into 16:8 over 7 days. That's the protocol the evidence backs for beginners, and it's the starting point for everything else.
