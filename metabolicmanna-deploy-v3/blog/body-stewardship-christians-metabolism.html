Body stewardship in 2026 — what Christians owe their metabolism.

8 min read · Published April 14, 2026 · Faith cluster
The short answer

1 Corinthians 6:19 calls the body a "temple of the Holy Spirit," which most Christian wellness content reads as a slogan. It isn't. In a time when the food environment actively damages metabolic health, caring for your body has become an act of stewardship — the same theological category as caring for money, time, or creation. Metabolic health is not vanity. It is obedience.

There is a strange gap in modern Christian life. We have extensive, careful theology about how we spend our money, our time, our influence. We have almost nothing careful about how we take care of our bodies — except, maybe, a vague sense that we should "be healthy" and a slightly embarrassed awareness that the church has usually treated the body as either sinful or irrelevant.

Meanwhile, the food environment in 2026 is actively damaging the metabolic health of nearly every adult in the developed world. Processed foods, refined carbs, sedentary work, fragmented sleep, constant snacking — these are not neutral. They reshape the body over time, and the reshaping is quietly catastrophic. Type 2 diabetes, fatty liver disease, insulin resistance, and early cardiovascular damage are arriving earlier and at higher rates than at any point in recorded history.

This post is an argument that Christians — specifically — should have something to say about that. Not because metabolic health is the highest good (it isn't). But because if the body is what the scripture says it is, then treating it like it doesn't matter is its own kind of theological error.

What the scripture actually says about the body.

The foundational passage is 1 Corinthians 6:19–20, where Paul writes to a church in a city full of philosophical movements that treated the body as either an object of worship or an obstacle to overcome:

1 Corinthians 6:19–20 (NIV)
"Do you not know that your bodies are temples of the Holy Spirit, who is in you, whom you have received from God? You are not your own; you were bought at a price. Therefore honor God with your bodies."

This passage is one of the most quoted and one of the most domesticated in modern Christian wellness culture. It gets stitched onto devotionals about not getting tattoos, or eating "clean," or avoiding certain substances. But the actual theological content is broader and more interesting than any of those readings.

Paul's claim is that the body is not a container you happen to be riding in. It is not morally neutral. It is not a problem to be overcome. It is a dwelling place — the actual location where the Spirit of God is present in a Christian. That is a remarkable statement, and the implications run in both directions. It elevates the body (what happens to it matters) and it constrains how you're supposed to treat it (this isn't yours to trash).

Two other passages make the same move from different angles:

  • Romans 12:1 — "Offer your bodies as a living sacrifice, holy and pleasing to God — this is your true and proper worship." The body is the primary medium of worship in Paul's framing, not a distraction from it.
  • 1 Corinthians 10:31 — "So whether you eat or drink or whatever you do, do it all for the glory of God." Eating — the most basic daily bodily act — is specifically named as a domain where Christians are supposed to be intentional.

Put together, these passages don't give us a diet plan. They give us a framework: the body is a stewardship, eating is worship, and whatever damages the body casually is a small ongoing violation of both.

What "stewardship" actually means.

In the broader theology of stewardship — the way Christians have talked about money, time, creation, and influence for centuries — a few principles consistently apply:

  1. It's not yours. Whatever you steward, you don't ultimately own. You're holding it on behalf of someone who does.
  2. You will be asked about it. Stewardship implies accountability. Something is expected of you — not as a perfectionist performance, but as a response to a trust.
  3. Damage is not neutral. You can't treat something as "yours to waste" if it isn't yours. Neglect is a category of harm, not just absence.
  4. It's for others as much as for you. Stewardship is never purely self-directed. What you do with what you have affects the people around you.

Now apply these four to the body — specifically, to metabolic health. It's not yours: the scripture says it belongs to God. You will be asked about it: Romans 12:1 frames it as worship. Damage is not neutral: neglect has costs, and those costs compound. It's for others: a body that breaks down early takes you out of the lives of the people who depend on you.

If any of those things are true, then chronic metabolic damage — the kind caused by a diet of ultra-processed food, a sedentary lifestyle, poor sleep, and constant glucose spikes — is not a neutral lifestyle choice. It is something closer to a small, daily violation of a trust. Said more directly: it matters theologically, not just medically.

Metabolic health is not vanity. It is stewardship.

What the research says about the cost of not doing this.

The stakes aren't abstract. Consider what has happened to metabolic health in the developed world over the last fifty years:

  • Type 2 diabetes prevalence has more than tripled since 1980
  • Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease now affects roughly 30% of US adults
  • Insulin resistance has become so common that by some measures, fewer than 12% of American adults are metabolically healthy
  • Cardiovascular disease remains the leading cause of death globally, and the majority of it is driven by metabolic dysfunction that starts decades earlier

These outcomes don't happen by accident. They happen because the modern food environment is misaligned with human biology in ways that were not possible even fifty years ago. Ultra-processed foods are engineered to be hyperpalatable. Added sugars are ubiquitous. Sedentary work is the default. Late-night eating disrupts circadian metabolism. All of these were edge cases in 1975. All of them are the default in 2026.

A Christian who ignores this is not living "above the world." They are absorbing the damage of the world — slowly, invisibly, on the assumption that the body is not part of their spiritual life. Paul would disagree.

What stewardship looks like in practice.

This is not a call to perfectionism, and it is not a diet plan. It's a shift in framing. If you take the stewardship framework seriously, the practical implications look something like this:

  1. Eat mostly whole food. Not exclusively. Not ritually. Not with guilt about imperfection. Just — most of the time, most of the meals, eat food that was food recently, in a form your great-grandmother would recognize as food. This one change produces most of the metabolic benefit of every expensive intervention the wellness industry sells.
  2. Respect circadian rhythms. Eat during daylight when possible. Sleep when it's dark. Avoid late-night eating — the research is clear that it disrupts insulin response (see our post on insulin sensitivity).
  3. Move. Not necessarily "exercise" in the gym-based sense. Walk after meals (the research on this is excellent). Take stairs. Carry things. The body is built for motion and withers without it.
  4. Use tools like intermittent fasting when they fit — as disciplines, not as identities. A 16:8 window can be a useful stewardship practice. So can a Daniel Fast. So can simply eating breakfast earlier. See our beginner post for one path.
  5. Sleep. This one gets neglected in nutrition-focused wellness content but is arguably the highest-leverage stewardship practice there is. Poor sleep wrecks everything downstream.
  6. Measure the right things. Not scale weight alone. Fasting glucose, lipid panels, blood pressure, HbA1c if indicated — these are the outputs that actually correlate with long-term health and longevity. Get them checked.

What this list is not: a purity code, a moral rubric, or a badge for spiritual superiority. Stewardship is not performance. It is care. The opposite of neglect is not perfectionism — it is attention.

The honest caveats.

The body is not the highest good.

If this framework turns into an obsession with metabolic markers, or a license to judge other people's eating, or a substitute for the actual spiritual life it's supposed to serve — it has failed. The scripture is clear that the body is a temple, not the occupant. The occupant is the point. Stewardship is supposed to support the life of the Spirit, not compete with it.

This is not a prosperity gospel of health.

Some people take care of their bodies diligently and get cancer anyway. Some people neglect them for decades and live to 95. Health outcomes are not a moral ledger. This post is not saying that disease is punishment for poor stewardship, or that wellness is a sign of righteousness. It is only saying that the framework of care matters, independent of whether any particular outcome follows.

Isaiah 58 still applies.

If your metabolic stewardship is making you a worse person to live with — more rigid, more judgmental, more self-focused — Isaiah 58 says the same thing about this that it says about fasting. The practice has to fruit in kindness or it isn't working.

The honest bottom line.

In 2026, taking care of your metabolism is not optional in the way it was for your grandparents. The food environment has changed too much. The default lifestyle does too much damage. The question is whether you want to call that stewardship or just call it "being healthy" — and for Christians specifically, the theological category matters. Stewardship has implications. "Being healthy" has none.

The scripture says the body is a temple, that it was bought at a price, and that how you treat it is part of your worship. If any of that is true, then metabolic health stops being a lifestyle preference and becomes something more ordinary and more serious: a small, ongoing obedience. Not glamorous. Not moralized. Just daily care for something that belongs to someone else and is worth the care.
